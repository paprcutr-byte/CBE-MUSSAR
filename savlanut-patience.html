The Middah

What Is Savlanut?

Savlanut is more than waiting. It comes from the root sevel — to bear a burden. Patience, in the Mussar sense, is the capacity to carry something heavy without being crushed by it.

This is not passive resignation. It is an active, conscious choice to hold discomfort — a delay, a difficult person, an unresolved situation — with dignity and without reactive speech or action.

“The patient in spirit is better than the proud in spirit.”
— Kohelet 7:8

American Mussar asks: where on the spectrum is your patience? Not just in obvious moments of frustration, but in small daily irritations — traffic, technology, waiting, being misunderstood.

Hebrew Root

ס-ב-ל

Sevel — to carry, to bear a load. Patience is not about waiting passively. It is about what you do with the weight while you wait.

Erech Apayim

Long-Nosed

The Hebrew idiom for patience: “long-nosed,” as opposed to short-tempered. Draw out the breath and pause before reacting.

Connection

Bridge to Sh'tikah

Savlanut and Silence are deeply linked. The patience to wait often requires the silence to not fill the gap with reactive words.

Pirkei Avot

Strength of Spirit

“Who is mighty? One who subdues their evil inclination.” The Mishnah locates true strength not in force but in self-mastery.

Daily Practice

Mantra & Practice Structure

Morning Mantra — Write on a card by your bed

“This too shall pass, and I have the strength to bear the burden until it does.”

Also: Erech apayim — draw out the breath and pause before reacting. Every person has their hour, and everything has its place.

1
Morning — Read the mantra aloud

Before checking your phone, read the mantra. Set an intention to notice one moment of patience or impatience today.

2
Throughout the day — Notice and pause

When impatience arises, name it silently: “There is my impatience.” Then breathe and ask: is this about the situation, or my need to control it?

3
Evening — Journal one observation

Use the daily prompt below. A few sentences is enough. Write what you noticed, not what you should have done.

The Spectrum

Where Is My Patience?

Savlanut lives on a spectrum. The goal is the balanced middle — bearing burdens without either passive resignation or reactive impatience.

Too Much PatienceBalanced SavlanutToo Little Patience
Passive · Suppresses needs
Becomes a doormatBearing with dignity
Pauses before reacting
Holds without resentmentReactive · Short-fused
Impatient speech
Explosive frustration
Deficiency

Staying silent when you need to speak up. Accepting mistreatment. Becoming so “patient” that you lose yourself in the process.

Excess

Explosive anger, harsh reactive speech, intolerance of small frustrations, treating delay as personal affront.

“The patient in spirit is better than the proud in spirit.”

— Kohelet 7:8

“Whoever is slow to anger has great understanding, but one who has a hasty temper exalts folly.”

— Proverbs 14:29

3May 31Day 3

How does savlanut differ from passive resignation? What does it mean to carry a burden with dignity rather than simply give up?

“Be still before the Lord and wait patiently for Him.”

— Psalm 37:7

“Who is mighty? One who subdues their evil inclination.”

— Pirkei Avot 4:1

“A patient man calms strife.”

— Proverbs 15:18

6Jun 3Day 6

— Kohelet 7:8

How has your “patience muscle” changed? Where did you notice improvement — even briefly?
