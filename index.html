Kimmutz
Frugality

Two weeks working the soul trait of frugality — mindful sufficiency, wise stewardship of resources, and contentment with what we already have.

Path A: Full Practice Path B: Integration
קימוץ
The Middah

What Is Kimmutz?

In Mussar, Frugality is not deprivation. It is the practice of living with exactly what we need — treating our resources as a blessing rather than a possession to hoard or squander.

The soul trait of Kimmutz governs our relationship to money, time, food, and energy. It sits at a balance point between two extremes: stinginess, which withholds even from real need, and thoughtless excess, which wastes what could serve others.

Be careful with your money. Do not spend even a penny needlessly, and do not withhold spending on anything that is truly needed.
— Rabbi Mendel of Satanov, Cheshbon HaNefesh

The Cheshbon HaNefesh offers the foundational Mussar practice for this middah: a nightly accounting of the soul, noticing where money or resources were used well, and where they were not. This single daily habit is the entire practice in miniature.

Hebrew Root

ק-מ-ץ

K-m-tz. To grasp a handful, to gather in a closed hand. Connected to the idea of taking precisely enough — not more, not less.

Bitachon

Trust as Foundation

Frugality is paired with trust. Recognizing that we are provided for eases the anxiety that often drives impulsive acquiring or hoarding.

Hakarat Ha'tov

Gratitude for What Is

Noticing what we already have quiets the restless wanting that frugality is meant to address. Contentment, not scarcity, is the goal.

Stewardship

Resources as Trust

What we have — time, money, energy — is held in trust, not owned outright. Waste and hoarding are both failures of stewardship.

Daily Practice

Mantra & Practice Structure

American Mussar practice has three parts: morning mantra, mindful action throughout the day, and evening journaling.

Morning Mantra — Write on a card by your bed

Today I will use exactly what I need, waste nothing, and notice the blessing in what I already have.

Drawn from Cheshbon HaNefesh: "Be careful with your money. Do not spend even a penny needlessly, and do not withhold spending on anything that is truly needed."

The Three-Part Daily Structure

1
Morning Check-In

Before the day begins, name one resource — money, food, time — you intend to use mindfully today.

2
The Pause Before Purchase

Before any non-essential spending, pause and ask: Do I need this, or do I simply want it right now?

3
Evening Journal

Review the day's spending and waste. Note any impulsive purchase or discarded resource — and one moment of gratitude for what sufficed.

Key Texts on Frugality

Choose Your Path

Two Practice Paths

Choose the path that fits where you are — or weave them together in the second week.

Path A

Work Kimmutz for the full two weeks. Use the mantra, the daily practice, the weekly prompts below, and the tracker.

  • Morning: name one resource to use mindfully today
  • Throughout the day: the pause before non-essential purchases
  • Weekly: try the One-Week Challenge described below
  • Evening: journal one observation about spending or waste each day
Path B: Weaving It In

Connect Kimmutz to Savlanut and Sh'tikah. Notice how patience and silence already touch how you spend, waste, and want.

⏳
Kimmutz + Savlanut
Frugality Needs Patience
Impulsive spending is often impatience in disguise. Where could a moment of Savlanut prevent an unnecessary purchase?
🤐
Kimmutz + Sh'tikah
Silence Before Spending
Just as Sh'tikah asks us to pause before speaking, Kimmutz asks us to pause before acquiring. What would a 3-second rule for spending look like?
Two-Week Prompts

Torah Prompts — Two Weeks of Kimmutz

One text, one theme, and one practice focus per week.

Week 1: Noticing Enough
July 10 – 16
Cheshbon HaNefesh
Be careful with your money. Do not spend even a penny needlessly, and do not withhold spending on anything that is truly needed.
  • Where this week did you spend or use something without thinking?
  • What is the difference between needing something and wanting it right now?
  • Where have you been withholding even from a real need — your own or someone else's?
  • What is one resource in your life you tend to take for granted?
Week 1 Practice

The One-Week Challenge. Choose a single area of daily consumption — coffee, food waste, a subscription, impulse purchases — and track it closely every day.

Week 2: Gratitude & Restraint
July 17 – 23
Hakarat Ha'tov & Bitachon
Recognizing that you are provided for helps alleviate the anxiety or deprivation that often drives impulsive buying.
  • What did tracking your consumption this week reveal about your habits?
  • Where did gratitude — rather than anxiety — shape a choice about money or resources?
  • Did you find yourself hoarding or over-restricting anywhere? What was underneath that?
  • What would it feel like to trust that you have "enough" without checking again?
Week 2 Practice

Pair Kimmutz with Gratitude. Each evening, name one thing you already own or already have that fully satisfies its purpose — no upgrade needed.
